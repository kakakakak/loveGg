<li>caching</li>

<td class="answercell">
    <div class="post-text" itemprop="text">
<p>You can improve I/O performance in several ways depending upon what you use for your storage setup:</p>

<ol>
<li>Increase filesystem block size if your app displays good spatial locality in its I/Os or uses large files.</li>
<li>Use <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/RAID#RAID_10" rel="nofollow">RAID 10</a> (striping + mirroring) for performance + redundancy (disk failure protection).</li>
<li>Use fast disks (Performance Wise: SSD > FC > SATA).</li>
<li>Segregate workloads at different times of day. e.g. Backup during night, normal app I/O during day.</li>
<li>Turn off <a href="http://lwn.net/Articles/244829/" rel="nofollow">atime updates</a> in your filesystem.</li>
<li>Cache NFS file handles a.k.a. <a href="http://www.niallkennedy.com/blog/2009/04/facebook-haystack.html" rel="nofollow">Haystack</a> (Facebook), if storing data on NFS server.</li>
<li>Combine small files into larger chunks, a.k.a <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BigTable" rel="nofollow">BigTable</a>, <a href="http://hadoop.apache.org/hbase/" rel="nofollow">HBase</a>.</li>
<li>Avoid very large directories i.e. lots of files in the same directory (instead divide files between different directories in a hierarchy).</li>
<li>Use a <a href="http://www.netapp.com/in/products/storage-systems/data-ontap-gx/" rel="nofollow">clustered</a> <a href="http://blogs.netapp.com/dave/2006/06/ontap_gxpast_an.html" rel="nofollow">storage system</a> (yeah not exactly commodity hardware).</li>
<li>Optimize/design your application for sequential disk accesses whenever possible.</li>
<li>Use <a href="http://memcached.org/" rel="nofollow">memcached</a>. :)</li>
</ol>

<p>You may want to look at "Lessons Learned" section of <a href="http://highscalability.com/blog/2009/8/5/stack-overflow-architecture.html" rel="nofollow">StackOverflow Architecture</a>.</p>

<td class="answercell">
    <div class="post-text" itemprop="text">
<p>1 million requests per day is 12/second. Stack overflow is small enough that you could (with interesting normalization and compression tricks) fit it entirely in RAM of a 64 GByte Dell PowerEdge 2970. I'm not sure where caching and replication should play a role.</p>

<p>If you have a problem thinking enough about normalization, a PowerEdge R900 with 256GB is available.</p>

<p>If you don't like a single point of failure, you can connect a few of those and just push updates over a socket (preferably on a separate network card). Even a peak load of 12K/second should not be a problem for a main-memory system.</p>

<p>The best way to avoid the I/O bottleneck is to not do I/O (as much as possible). That means a prevayler-like architecture with batched writes (no problem to lose a few seconds of data), basically a log file, and for replication also write them out to a socket.</p>
