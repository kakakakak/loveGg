<p>I am trying to find out the most common word in a text using Hadoop. Hadoop is is a framework that allows for the distributed processing of large data sets across clusters of computers.</p>

<p>I know this can be done easily by using the Unix command :  <code>job: sort -n -k2 txtname | tail</code>. But this doesn't scale to large datasets. So I'm trying to break the problem up and then combine the results.</p>

       runJob(Arrays.copyOfRange(args, 0, args.length-1), args[args.length-1]);
  }
}
</code></pre>

<td class="answercell">
    <div class="post-text" itemprop="text">
<p>This is the canonical word count problem, you can google and find any number of solutions for basic word count. Then you have just one more step to do: return the word with the largest count.  </p>

<p>How to do that?  </p>

<p>If the amount of data is not too large and you can afford to use a single reducer then set the number of reducers to 1. Inside your reduce keep a local variable that remembers which of the groups (i.e. words) has/have the highest count(s). Then write that result to a file in HDFS.</p>

<p>If the amount of data precludes using a single reducer then you just have one extra step beyond the first one mentioned above: you need to find the highest count across all of the reducers. You can do that by either a global counter or by writing each of the individual max words to their own (tiny) file in hdfs and have a post processing step (probably a linux script) to then parse and obtain the max of the maxes.  Alternatively you could have yet another map/reduce job find it - but that would be a bit of overkill for that small/ simple operation.</p>

<td class="answercell">
    <div class="post-text" itemprop="text">
<p>I think you are mistaken that <code>sort -n -k2</code> will not work at scale in this case. WordCount will probably never output on the order of thousands or tens of thousands of words. This is simply due to the cardinality of most natural languages. So, even if you have 10 Petabytes of data, it will still be distilled down into at most 10,000 or 20,000 words (albeit the counts are high). </p>

<p>First, have your WordCount job run on the data. Then, use some bash to pull the top N out.</p>

<pre><code>hadoop fs -cat /output/of/wordcount/part* | sort -n -k2 | tail -n20
</code></pre>

<hr>

<p>If for some reason you are getting a ton of words out of wordcount (i.e., you aren't doing it on natural language)...</p>

<p>Have two MapReduce jobs:</p>

<ol>
<li>WordCount: counts all the words (pretty much the example exactly)</li>
<li>TopN: A MapReduce job that finds the top N of something (here are some examples: <a href="https://github.com/adamjshook/mapreducepatterns/blob/master/MRDP/src/main/java/mrdp/ch3/TopTenDriver.java" rel="nofollow">source code</a>, <a href="http://blog.gopivotal.com/products/how-hadoop-mapreduce-can-transform-how-you-build-top-ten-lists" rel="nofollow">blog post</a>)</li>
</ol>

<p>Have the output of WordCount write to HDFS. Then, have TopN read that output. This is called job chaining and there are a number of ways to solve this problem: oozie, bash scripts, firing two jobs from your driver, etc.</p>

<p>The reason you need two jobs is you are doing two aggregations: one is word count, and the second is topN. Typically in MapReduce each aggregation requires its own MapReduce job.</p>
