<p>if I wanted to create a distributed database for media files (say, images), what would I use?</p>

<p>The idea is that the DB wouldn't be controllable by any particular entity (though the software would be, though it's GPL / forkable).</p>

<p>Instead, you'd have a client which has something like libtorrent and can be used to browse / cache the database.   You'd configure the client with a list of 'sources'.   </p>

<p>Cydia is a good model for this. </p>

<p>Though instead of using web hosts, I'd like to use bit torrent seeders that act as super nodes (which would catalog sub nodes of arbitrary tree height)</p>

<p>Would libtorrent be the best way to go?  Or would there be a better alternative?</p>

<td class="answercell">
    <div class="post-text" itemprop="text">
<p>Just because there exist seeders for a given torrent doesn't mean <em>your</em> client is aware of what they are seeding. You would need at least a magnet link, or a torrent file for each separate "thing".</p>

<p>So, "caching" content with torrent, ok, but as far as "browsing" is concerned, you need something else (eg: an index, that would list magnet/torrent files - and I doubt you would build that using lib/bittorrent...).</p>

<p>Secondly, your list of "sources" would be provided by a tracker in order to connect to your swarm, or at least a list of known peers (supernodes as you call them) if using DHT - yet again provided by something else that libtorrent.</p>

<p>Also, obviously (?) you can't "update" content with torrent - if something "changes", then it's a different torrent - so, short term, you would have a database with items that can't be modified... "replicated" over bittorrent, but indexed by some other technology.</p>

<p>I don't quite understand why you are looking at bittorrent to (re)implement a "distributed database", but this seems ill-formed to me (if only, because of the index, that wouldn't be distributed, pretty much defeating the purpose).</p>

<p>If your problem is reliability / scalability, why not consider any (existing) "db" engine that would natively do the job? (replica sets with mongo, or distributed filesystem on hadoop, or even good old-school handmade data replication between nodes, or even sql master slaves, or some cloud storage...).</p>

<p>If your problem is "ownership" over the database, yet again it's not about the content, it's about the index.
Now, I can imagine something where peers would exchange lists of items they own between them, allowing a given peer to get data from another given one, but this is quite far away from what I would call a "database"...</p>

<p>I don't think you can let-go "ownership" (at least over the index) without letting go the concept of "database"... (assuming I understood well what you meant by "the DB wouldn't be controllable by any particular entity")</p>
