<td class="answercell">
    <div class="post-text" itemprop="text">
<p>You have two obvious options:</p>

<hr>

<p>Have two MapReduce jobs:</p>

<ol>
<li>WordCount: counts all the words (pretty much the example exactly)</li>
<li>TopN: A MapReduce job that finds the top N of something (here are some examples: <a href="https://github.com/adamjshook/mapreducepatterns/blob/master/MRDP/src/main/java/mrdp/ch3/TopTenDriver.java">source code</a>, <a href="http://blog.gopivotal.com/products/how-hadoop-mapreduce-can-transform-how-you-build-top-ten-lists">blog post</a>)</li>
</ol>

<p>Have the output of WordCount write to HDFS. Then, have TopN read that output. This is called job chaining and there are a number of ways to solve this problem: oozie, bash scripts, firing two jobs from your driver, etc.</p>

<p>The reason you need two jobs is you are doing two aggregations: one is word count, and the second is topN. Typically in MapReduce each aggregation requires its own MapReduce job.</p>

<hr>

<p>First, have your WordCount job run on the data. Then, use some bash to pull the top N out.</p>

<pre><code>hadoop fs -cat /output/of/wordcount/part* | sort -n -k2 -r | head -n20
</code></pre>

<p><code>sort -n -k2 -r</code> says "sort numerically by column #2, in descending order". <code>head -n20</code> pulls the top twenty.</p>

<p>This is the better option for WordCount, just because WordCount will probably only output on the order of thousands or tens of thousands of lines and you don't need a MapReduce job for that. Remember that just because you have hadoop around doesn't mean you should solve all your problems with Hadoop.</p>

<hr>

<p>One non-obvious version, which is tricky but a mix of both of the above...</p>

<p>Write a WordCount MapReduce job, but in the Reducer do something like in the TopN MapReduce jobs I showed you earlier. Then, have each reducer output only the TopN results from that reducer.</p>

<p>So, if you are doing Top 10, each reducer will output 10 results. Let's say you have 30 reducers, you'll output 300 results.</p>

<p>Then, do the same thing as in option #2 with bash:</p>

<pre><code>hadoop fs -cat /output/of/wordcount/part* | sort -n -k2 -r | head -n10
</code></pre>

<p>This should be faster because you are only postprocessing a fraction of the results.</p>

<p>This is the fastest way I can think of doing this, but it's probably not worth the effort.</p>
