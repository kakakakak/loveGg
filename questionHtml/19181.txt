    <div class="post-text" itemprop="text">

<p>I'm currently investigating how to store and analyze enriched time based data with up to 1000 columns per line. At the moment Cassandra together with either Solr, Hadoop or Spark offered by Datastax Enterprise seem to fulfill my requirements on the rough. But the devil is in the detail.</p>

<p>Out of the 1000 columns about 60 are used for real-time-like queries (web-frontend, user sends form and expect quick response). These queries are more or less GROUPBY statements where the number or occurrences are counted.</p>

<p>As Cassandra itself does not provide the required analytical capabilities (no GROUPBY), I'm left these alternatives:</p>

<ul>
<li>Roughly query via Cassandra and filter the resultset within self-written code</li>
<li>Index the data with Solr and run facet.pivot queries</li>
<li>Use either Hadoop or Spark and run the queries</li>
</ul>

<p>The first approach seems cumbersome and prone to errors… Solr does have some anayltic features but without multifield grouping I'm stuck with pivots. I don't know whether this is a good or performant approach though… Last but not least there are Hadoop and Spark, the prior known not to be the best for real-time queries, the later pretty new and maybe not production ready.</p>

<p>So which way to go? There is no one-fits-all here, but before I go one way through I'd like to get some feedback. Maybe I'm thinking to complex or my expectations are too high :S</p>

<p>Thanks in advance,</p>

<p>Arman</p>

    <div class="post-text" itemprop="text">
<p>I would recommend Spark, if you take a loot at the <a href="https://cwiki.apache.org/confluence/display/SPARK/Powered+By+Spark" rel="nofollow">list of companies using it</a> you'll such names as <strong>Amazon, eBay and Yahoo!</strong>. Also, as you noted in the comment, it's becoming a mature tool.</p>

<p>You've given arguments against Cassandra and Solr already, so I'll focus on explaining why Hadoop MapReduce wouldn't do as well as Spark for <em>real-time</em> queries.</p>

<p>Hadoop and <strong>MapReduce</strong> were <strong>designed to leverage hard disk</strong> under the assumption that for big data IO is negligible. As a result data are read and wrote at least twice - in map stage and in reduce stage. This allows you to recover from failures as partial result are secured but it that's not want you want when aiming for <em>real-time</em> queries.</p>

<p><strong>Spark</strong> not only aims to fix MapReduce shortcomings, it also focuses on interactive data analysis, which is exactly what you want. This goal is achieved mainly by <strong>utilizing RAM</strong> and the results are astonishing. Spark jobs will often be <strong>10-100 times faster</strong> than MapReduce equivalents.</p>

<p>The only caveat is the amount of memory you have. Most probably <strong>your data is probably going to feat in the RAM</strong> you can provide <strong>or you can rely on sampling</strong>. Usually when interactively working with data there is no real need to use MapReduce and it seems to be so in your case.</p>
