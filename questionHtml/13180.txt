    <div class="post-text" itemprop="text">

<p>I thought about it and technically you could use databases tables and temporary tables/database views directly from a client.</p>

<ol>
<li>So wouldn't it be more efficient for the client to interact directly
with the database as opposed to using a tiered architecture?</li>
<li>What are the cons?</li>
<li>Are there any libraries/frameworks in Java that help do this
already?</li>
</ol>

<p>Here is a demo model that I have created to demonstrate a high-level working of my proposal:</p>

<p><img src="http://i.stack.imgur.com/rR91T.png" alt="Cloud-Based Content &amp; Transport"></p>

<p>Some of the Benefits of this model would include:</p>

<ol>
<li>No Guru knowledge of Web Services, XML, JMS and more will be
required to interact with other systems and platforms. This also means that new graduates will be able to learn more about other, more beneficial things, rather than learning something that is so vast and yet offers so little in contrast to what we <strong>already</strong> have.</li>
<li>Security concerns that would normally be left with architects and
developers when making a web service will now be left with DB server
providers like MySQL, PostgreSQL, etc.</li>
<li>No more outdated transport mechanisms as all transport will be handled by DB server providers. This will also attribute to better scalability and more competition between enterprise DB server providers.</li>
<li>Replication of methodology when connecting to other systems. Less
time spent studying the data you retrieve and how you retrieve it.
The "usual steps" could be a simple publication of connection url,
username, password instead of a bunch of urls and what data they
contain and how you use the data in terms of processing and security.</li>
</ol>

<td class="answercell">
    <div class="post-text" itemprop="text">
<p>The problem with using the database... So many issues here.  Not all platforms have free databases available.  Access to mainframe based services is always an issue, and not only technical, but cost.  You want to access a Mainframe based DB, they are going to charge you for every single client.  </p>

<p>What happens when you change databases?  Maybe move to in-memory or a Hadoop solution, you need to rewrite your client software to deal with it, since they are so tightly interwoven. With a Web Service, the middle tier changes, but not the interface between the client. </p>

<p>How do you manage resources?  In a web service, you can limit the number of threads running, with a database, your clients could potentially bring the server to it's knees and there's not much you can do about it.  </p>

<p>In most enterprise-wide shops, the databases are not free.  The database vendors would love your solution, as they would love to charge you for all those connections. </p>

<p>Scalability... your pretty much limited to database scaling solutions. </p>

<p>Is your solution a fit for some number of applications?  Absolutely, it really depends on your goals and the scope of the project your working on.  </p>
