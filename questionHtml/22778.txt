    <div class="post-text" itemprop="text">
<p>Hadoop has high latency, therefore you should not consider it for a real-time scenario.</p>

<p>Did you considered partition your databases, for example geographically? For example: one database for Germany, another for France, another for UK and another for the South of Europe.</p>

<p>What about an in-memory databases, it might be worth give them a try (or see if they do what do you want). They have fancy atomic operators, for example <a href="http://redis.io/" rel="nofollow">Redis</a> have something very similiar to the famous <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fetch-and-add" rel="nofollow">Fetch And Add</a>. </p>

<td class="answercell">
    <div class="post-text" itemprop="text">
<p>This is a quote from the famous Hadoop book: <a href="http://shop.oreilly.com/product/0636920021773.do" rel="nofollow">Hadoop: The Definitive Guide, 3rd Edition</a> (page 46)</p>

<blockquote>
  <p>Low-latency data access
  Applications that require low-latency access to data, in the tens of
  milliseconds range, will not work well with HDFS. Remember, HDFS is
  optimized for delivering a high throughput of data, and this may be at
  the expense of latency. HBase is currently a better choice for 
  low-latency access.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>Hbase is not using MapReduce (but MapReduce jobs can query HBase), this is a huge benefit in latency in compare to Hive/Pig. </p>

<p>Also, if you need some SQL-like interface, take a look on <a href="http://www.cloudera.com/content/cloudera/en/products-and-services/cdh/impala.html" rel="nofollow">Impala</a>. It's not using MapReduce too, but may be slower then HBase (better check this information with experiments)</p>
